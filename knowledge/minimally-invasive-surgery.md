Minimally invasive surgery (MIS) encompasses surgical techniques that limit the size of incisions needed, reducing tissue trauma, postoperative pain, and recovery time compared to traditional open surgery. The two primary forms are laparoscopic surgery and robotic-assisted surgery.

## Laparoscopic Surgery

Laparoscopic procedures use several small incisions through which a camera (laparoscope) and specialized long-handled instruments are inserted. The surgeon operates while viewing magnified images on a video monitor. This approach is the standard for many common procedures including [[cholecystectomy]] and [[inguinal-hernia]] repair.

## Robotic-Assisted Surgery

Robotic-assisted surgery builds on the laparoscopic approach by adding a robotic platform that translates the surgeon's hand movements into precise instrument movements. Systems like the da Vinci platform offer enhanced 3D visualization, greater instrument dexterity, and features like fluorescence imaging. See [[robotic-cholecystectomy]] for a detailed example.

## Advantages Over Open Surgery

Minimally invasive techniques generally offer smaller incisions and less scarring, reduced postoperative pain, shorter hospital stays, faster return to normal activities, and lower risk of wound infection. However, not all patients or conditions are suitable for MIS, and conversion to open surgery may sometimes be necessary.

## Applications in General Surgery

MIS techniques are widely used across general surgery, including gallbladder removal ([[cholecystectomy]]), hernia repair ([[inguinal-hernia]]), appendectomy, bariatric surgery, and colorectal procedures. The field continues to evolve with advances in robotic platforms, single-incision techniques, and imaging technologies.
